Introduction

You are soldiers. Not heroes of legend, not chosen ones—soldiers. You serve in the Vanguard, an elite military unit tasked with operations too dangerous or too delicate for regular forces.

Magic exists in this world, but it is rare. Court mages advise kings. Elite battlemages turn the tide of wars. Wealthy nobles sponsor talented children who show the spark. For most people—including you, for now—magic is something that happens to other people.

But the Vanguard doesn't recruit ordinary soldiers. Something in you caught their attention. You may not know what it is yet. Neither do we.

For now, you are exceptional in the way that skilled, trained, dangerous people are exceptional. What comes later... we'll discover together.

About This Document

This manual contains everything you need to create a character and begin play. It covers levels 1 and 2. Additional content will be revealed as you advance.

This game is built on D&D 5th Edition. Where rules aren't specified here, assume standard 5e rules apply—combat, conditions, equipment, and so on.

  • There are no 5e spellcasting classes (Wizard, Cleric, etc.). Magic exists but is rare.
  • The ten classes in this manual replace all 5e classes.
  • All characters are human.
  • Ability scores use the method described below, not rolling or point-buy.
  • Level 3+ features will be revealed as you advance.
  • How (and whether) your character might access different capabilities will become clear in time.
  • Advanced training options exist. You'll learn about them when the time is right.

The World You Know

You're soldiers, not scholars or politicians. You know what soldiers know—what you've been told, what you've seen, what everyone just understands.

Valdris — Your Nation

You serve King Aldric IV, who's ruled for forty years. He's old now, in poor health. Everyone knows Crown Princess Mira will inherit, though some say she's too reformist and others say she's too traditional. Politics. You're not paid to worry about it.

Valdris is stable. Prosperous. Rule of law mostly works. The military is professional—you defend, you don't conquer. That's what people say, anyway, and it's mostly true. You're proud to serve.

The Neighbors

Thyrea: The old rival. Three major wars over the past two centuries. Right now it's "cold peace"—trade happens, diplomats smile, but everyone knows another war is possible. Thyrea's an empire with its own proud traditions. They think they should lead the region. So do you.

The Kethani Federation: Broke away from Valdris sixty years ago after a brutal independence war. A bunch of city-states that hate empires and welcome refugees. Fiercely proud, increasingly unstable. Relations are... tense.

Vormun: Small, wealthy, neutral. Famous for banking and diplomacy. Both Valdris and Thyrea court their favor. When you need someone to negotiate peace, you call Vormun. When you need a loan, same thing.

The Sarthani Reaches: Former Valdric colonies, "independent" for forty years now but still economically tied to Valdris. Ongoing instability, occasional insurgencies. Complicated history. You probably know soldiers who've done tours there—peacekeeping, they call it.

The Vanguard

You serve in an elite unit. Getting recruited was an honor. Your family probably boasted. The Vanguard handles operations too dangerous or too sensitive for regular forces.

You're part of the Shield—the branch that handles civil threats and local crises. Criminals, dangerous individuals, things that would overwhelm local authorities. Real work. Real threats.

The Vanguard has a reputation for being incorruptible. You're proud of that, even if you know institutions are never perfect.

Magic — What You Know

Magic exists. You know that court mages advise nobles. You've heard stories of elite battlemages turning battles. Rare, powerful, not for people like you.

Some people show "the spark" young and get sponsored by wealthy families. Some join the military's mage corps. The Vanguard calls them "awakened." You'll probably encounter a few during your service—elite operatives with abilities beyond normal soldiers.

Most people never touch magic at all.

You know enough to do your job. You know who to salute, which borders are tense, and why your orders matter. The rest—politics, history, conspiracy—that's above your pay grade.

For now.

The Flashback Rule

Don't stress about your backstory.

In this game, you're encouraged to leave your character's history vague at the start. As the campaign unfolds and you learn more about the world, you can use a Flashback to declare that something was "always true" about your character—even if you're only deciding it now.

How Flashbacks Work

At any point during play, you can declare a Flashback—a retroactive detail about your character's past that becomes established fact. The GM has veto power over Flashbacks that contradict established events or would unbalance the game, but the goal is to say "yes" whenever possible.

Example Flashbacks

  • After meeting an NPC merchant: "I actually grew up in this town—I know this guy."
  • When the party discovers a religious conspiracy: "My sister joined this order years ago. I haven't heard from her since."
  • Upon finding a rare weapon: "I trained with one of these as a child. My mentor used one."
  • When entering a foreign city: "I spent a winter here once, running from something. I still have contacts."

Begin with just a name, a general attitude, and maybe one or two concrete details. Let the rest emerge through play. Your backstory is a collaboration between you, the GM, and the unfolding story.

Why This Rule Exists

You don't yet know what parts of your background will matter. The world is larger and stranger than you realize. By leaving your history flexible, you give yourself permission to discover connections as they become meaningful, rather than guessing what might be relevant before the campaign begins.

Character Creation

Race

All characters are human. The Vanguard recruits from humanity's ranks. Other options may be introduced later.

Ability Scores

Your six ability scores are determined by the following method:

Physical Stats (Strength, Dexterity, Constitution)

  • Assign 16 to one (your primary physical stat)
  • Assign 14 to one (your secondary)
  • The remaining one is 12

Mental Stats (Intelligence, Wisdom, Charisma)

  • Assign 14 to one (your primary mental stat)
  • The other two are 10

A mobile skirmisher might choose: Dex 16, Con 14, Str 12, Wis 14, Int 10, Cha 10—prioritizing agility and awareness.

A tough frontline soldier might choose: Str 16, Con 14, Dex 12, Cha 14, Int 10, Wis 10—emphasizing power and presence.

Base Proficiencies

All classes share these starting proficiencies:

Category Proficiency
Hit Die d8 (some classes gain bonus HP; see class description)
Armor Medium armor, shields
Weapons Simple weapons
Tools One tool proficiency of your choice
Languages Valdric + additional languages equal to your Intelligence modifier (minimum 0)*
Skills See your class, plus one additional skill of your choice

*At character creation, you only know Valdric (the common language of Valdris and neighboring nations). If your Intelligence modifier grants you additional language proficiencies, you don't need to choose them now. Instead, when a situation arises where knowing another language would be useful, you can declare via flashback that your character learned it previously.

The Ten Classes

The Vanguard organizes its soldiers into ten specialized roles based on aptitude and fighting style. Your class determines your starting abilities and how you'll grow as a soldier.

Each class has a distinct combat identity. Some are mobile, some are tough, some support allies, some deal devastating damage under the right conditions. Choose based on how you want to fight.

These are military designations, not mystical titles. A "Champion" is the soldier assigned to hold the line. A "Skirmisher" is the one sent to flank. A "Ranger" is the mobile sharpshooter. Your commanding officer assigns these roles based on aptitude and fighting style—they describe what you do, not who you are.

Champion
Tough protector who absorbs punishment and locks down enemies.

Level 1 Features

  • Gain +1 AC, +1 to hit on all attacks, and +1 maximum HP per level
  • Gain one additional reaction per round
  • Absorb (reaction): When you or an adjacent ally takes damage, that creature gains temporary HP equal to half the damage taken (after other reductions, rounded down)
  • Defensive Grip: Your opportunity attacks, when they hit, end the target's voluntary movement
Skills: Choose 1 from (Athletics, Survival) • Choose 1 from (Insight, Medicine) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Sentinel

Creatures provoke opportunity attacks from you when they attack someone other than you while in your reach. When you hit with an opportunity attack, the target's speed becomes 0 for the rest of the turn.

Fighter
Mobile striker who gains power through constant repositioning.

Level 1 Features

  • Gain +1 AC, +1 to hit on all attacks, and +1 maximum HP per level
  • Reposition: Move up to 10 feet without provoking opportunity attacks. Can be used as a bonus action on your turn, or as a reaction when a creature moves within 5 feet of you.
Skills: Choose 1 from (Athletics, Survival) • Choose 1 from (Acrobatics, Performance) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Battleflow

When you use Reposition, gain +1 AC and +1 to hit until the end of your next turn. This bonus does not stack with itself.

Ranger
Mobile ranged specialist who marks and kites targets.

Level 1 Features

  • Reposition: Move up to 10 feet without provoking opportunity attacks. Can be used as a bonus action on your turn, or as a reaction when a creature moves within 5 feet of you.
  • Steady Shot: When you forgo your normal movement for the turn, gain +2 to hit and +4 damage on all ranged attacks this turn
Skills: Choose 1 from (Acrobatics, Performance) • Choose 1 from (Investigation, Perception) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Quarry

When you hit with a Steady Shot attack, you may designate that creature as your Quarry. Your subsequent attacks against your Quarry deal +1d4 bonus damage per 2 levels. You also gain a bonus equal to half your level (rounded down) on checks to track your Quarry. The target remains your Quarry until it dies or you designate a new Quarry.

Investigator
Precision striker who studies targets for devastating attacks.

Level 1 Features

  • Steady Shot: When you forgo your normal movement for the turn, gain +2 to hit and +4 damage on all ranged attacks this turn
  • Gain one additional bonus action per round
  • Hide (bonus action): Take the Hide action
  • Distract (bonus action): An adjacent creature has disadvantage on its attacks and grants advantage to attackers. Lasts until end of your next turn or until the creature attacks or is attacked.
Skills: Choose 1 from (Investigation, Perception) • Choose 1 from (Stealth, Deception) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Study

(Bonus action, once per round) Choose a creature you can see within 30 feet. You learn that creature's AC. Your attacks against that creature deal +1d4 bonus damage per 2 levels until the end of your next turn.

If you Study a creature whose AC you already learned through Study, you also learn its damage vulnerabilities, resistances, and immunities, if any.

If you Study a creature whose vulnerabilities you already learned through Study, the creature's AC and saving throws against your attacks and effects are also reduced by 2 until the end of your next turn.

Operative
Slippery survivor with exceptional action economy and reactive defense.

Level 1 Features

  • Gain one additional bonus action per round
  • Hide (bonus action): Take the Hide action
  • Distract (bonus action): An adjacent creature has disadvantage on its attacks and grants advantage to attackers. Lasts until end of your next turn or until the creature attacks or is attacked.
  • Gain one additional reaction per round
  • Absorb (reaction): When you or an adjacent ally takes damage, that creature gains temporary HP equal to half the damage taken (after other reductions, rounded down)
  • Defensive Grip: Your opportunity attacks, when they hit, end the target's voluntary movement
Skills: Choose 1 from (Stealth, Deception) • Choose 1 from (Insight, Medicine) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Preemptive Strike

(1/round) When an enemy you can see is about to attack you, use your reaction to make an opportunity attack first. If you incapacitate the enemy, their attack doesn't happen. You can still use your other reaction(s) for other abilities.

Monk
Agile brawler who flows between offense and defense.

Level 1 Features

  • Gain one additional reaction per round
  • Absorb (reaction): When you or an adjacent ally takes damage, that creature gains temporary HP equal to half the damage taken (after other reductions, rounded down)
  • Defensive Grip: Your opportunity attacks, when they hit, end the target's voluntary movement
  • Reposition: Move up to 10 feet without provoking opportunity attacks. Can be used as a bonus action on your turn, or as a reaction when a creature moves within 5 feet of you.
Skills: Choose 1 from (Insight, Medicine) • Choose 1 from (Acrobatics, Performance) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Martial Artist

You can use any object as an improvised weapon (minimum 1d4 damage). Once per turn, you can use a bonus action to make an unarmed strike or improvised weapon attack, dealing 1d6 + your Dexterity modifier + half your level (rounded down) in damage.

Commander
Ranged leader who supports allies while contributing personal firepower.

Level 1 Features

  • Gain one additional reaction per round
  • Absorb (reaction): When you or an adjacent ally takes damage, that creature gains temporary HP equal to half the damage taken (after other reductions, rounded down)
  • Defensive Grip: Your opportunity attacks, when they hit, end the target's voluntary movement
  • Steady Shot: When you forgo your normal movement for the turn, gain +2 to hit and +4 damage on all ranged attacks this turn
Skills: Choose 1 from (Insight, Medicine) • Choose 1 from (Investigation, Perception) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Rally

(Reaction, 1/round) When an ally within 60 feet that you can see misses an attack roll, you can allow that ally to reroll the attack. They must use the new result. If the ally hits with the rerolled attack, you may immediately make one weapon attack against the same target.

Sniper
Stationary devastator with unmatched ranged accuracy.

Level 1 Features

  • Gain +1 AC, +1 to hit on all attacks, and +1 maximum HP per level
  • Steady Shot: When you forgo your normal movement for the turn, gain +2 to hit and +4 damage on all ranged attacks this turn
Skills: Choose 1 from (Athletics, Survival) • Choose 1 from (Investigation, Perception) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Deadeye

When using Steady Shot, your ranged attacks score a critical hit on a roll of 18-20, ignore half cover and three-quarters cover, and when you score a critical hit, you roll the weapon's damage dice one additional time.

Thug
Durable ambusher who creates their own opportunities.

Level 1 Features

  • Gain +1 AC, +1 to hit on all attacks, and +1 maximum HP per level
  • Gain one additional bonus action per round
  • Hide (bonus action): Take the Hide action
  • Distract (bonus action): An adjacent creature has disadvantage on its attacks and grants advantage to attackers. Lasts until end of your next turn or until the creature attacks or is attacked.
Skills: Choose 1 from (Athletics, Survival) • Choose 1 from (Stealth, Deception) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Pummel

When you hit a creature with a melee attack, your attacks against that creature deal +1d6 bonus damage per 2 levels until the end of your next turn.

Skirmisher
Highly mobile flanker who weaves in and out of combat.

Level 1 Features

  • Reposition: Move up to 10 feet without provoking opportunity attacks. Can be used as a bonus action on your turn, or as a reaction when a creature moves within 5 feet of you.
  • Gain one additional bonus action per round
  • Hide (bonus action): Take the Hide action
  • Distract (bonus action): An adjacent creature has disadvantage on its attacks and grants advantage to attackers. Lasts until end of your next turn or until the creature attacks or is attacked.
Skills: Choose 1 from (Acrobatics, Performance) • Choose 1 from (Stealth, Deception) • Choose 1 additional skill

Level 2 -- Flanking Strike

When you and an ally are flanking a creature (on opposite sides per the flanking rule), your attacks against that creature deal +1d6 bonus damage per 2 levels.

Level 2 Advancement

At level 2, you gain your class feature (listed above with each class) and choose one of the following proficiency options:

Proficiency Options (Choose One)

Option Benefit
Martial Weapon Gain proficiency with one martial weapon of your choice
Heavy Armor Gain proficiency with heavy armor
Unarmored Defense While wearing no armor, your AC equals 10 + your Dexterity modifier + your Wisdom modifier

Most soldiers opt for a martial weapon to expand their combat options. Heavy armor is favored by those who expect to hold the front line. Unarmored Defense suits those who rely on speed and awareness rather than steel.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will I get magic?

Maybe.

Are these classes balanced?

We'll see. These rules are playtesting—real combat will reveal what works and what doesn't. If something proves over- or underpowered in practice, we can adjust. Every class is designed to be viable, but balance is something we'll discover together at the table.

What if I want to change my class later?

Talk to the GM. The Vanguard does reassign soldiers based on aptitude. If your initial choice doesn't fit how you want to play, we'll find a solution.

How long will this campaign be?

This is designed as a long campaign, potentially reaching level 20. The story has depth and will evolve as you advance. What you know at level 1 is just the beginning.

What's the expected tone?

Military competence with escalating stakes. You start as elite soldiers. You won't stay that simple. Expect your understanding of the world—and your place in it—to shift as the campaign progresses.

How much backstory should I write?

Not much. A name, an attitude, maybe one or two concrete details. Use the Flashback rule to fill in the rest as it becomes relevant. You don't yet know what parts of your history will matter.
